1. Assess Your Power Requirements#

Before embarking on your shopping journey, it’s crucial to determine the amount of backup power you need. Consider the essential appliances and electronics you want to keep running during an outage. Make a list of these necessities and calculate their total wattage. Add a 20% buffer to account for surge power and future additions. This careful assessment ensures you select a generator that meets your specific power requirements.

2. Choose the Right Fuel Type#

Small generators primarily operate on three fuel types: gasoline, propane, and diesel. Each fuel offers unique advantages and considerations:

  • Gasoline: Widely available, affordable, and easy to store, making it a popular choice. However, it’s flammable and requires careful handling.

  • Propane: Clean-burning and generates fewer emissions compared to gasoline. It’s also safer to store and handle, making it a great option for indoor use.

  • Diesel: Known for its fuel efficiency and longer run times, diesel generators are ideal for extended outages. However, they tend to be more expensive and require specialized maintenance.

3. Consider Portability and Noise Level#

If you plan on moving your generator around, portability is a key factor. Look for lightweight and compact models with wheels or carrying handles for easy transportation. Additionally, consider the noise level of the generator. Opt for models with low decibel ratings, especially if you’ll be using it near residential areas or during nighttime.

4. Safety Features Matter#

When dealing with generators, safety should always be your top priority. Look for models equipped with safety features like automatic voltage regulation (AVR), which prevents voltage fluctuations that can damage your appliances. Ground-fault circuit interrupters (GFCIs) provide protection against electrical shocks, while overload protection safeguards the generator from damage due to excessive power draw.

5. Read Reviews and Compare Prices#

Additional Tips for Using Small Generators#

  • Always operate the generator outdoors in a well-ventilated area to prevent carbon monoxide buildup.

  • Never connect the generator directly to your home’s electrical system without proper transfer switches installed by a qualified electrician.

  • Keep the generator dry and protected from rain or snow.

  • Store fuel in approved containers and never refuel a running generator.

  • Follow the manufacturer’s instructions for maintenance and servicing to ensure optimal performance and longevity.
